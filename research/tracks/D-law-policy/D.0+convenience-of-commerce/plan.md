# Paper D.0: Solar Fit Is Not Enough

## Research Question

How should a time-zone redesign tool incorporate DOT's convenience-of-commerce
standard alongside solar-noon fit, and what data proxies are needed before a
candidate map can be described as feasible rather than merely solar-aligned?

### Hypotheses / Claims

- **H1**: Solar-noon error alone is insufficient to justify US time-zone boundary
  changes under DOT's current convenience-of-commerce standard.
- **H2**: A practical feasibility layer can be built from source-backed proxies:
  commuting, media markets, transportation access, health care, schools,
  shopping, economic ties, and adjacent-zone device confusion.
- **H3**: Some high-solar-error counties will be poor reform candidates because
  convenience-of-commerce ties point toward the current zone.

Falsifiers:

- H1 fails if DOT guidance and rulemaking examples rely primarily on solar-time
  alignment rather than convenience-of-commerce evidence.
- H2 fails if the required proxy data cannot be sourced at county or comparable
  administrative-unit scale.
- H3 fails if high-solar-error counties consistently align with commerce
  proxies favoring reassignment.

### Scope Boundary

- **In scope**: US DOT process, Uniform Time Act/DST authority, Federal Register
  case examples, county-scale proxy design.
- **Out of scope**: legal advice, final recommendations, non-US legal systems.
- **Generalizability claim**: This paper addresses US DOT constraints only.

## Target Venue

- Venue: transportation policy / public administration / policy methods.
- Expected contribution type: legal-policy measurement framework.

## Methodology

- Summarize DOT time-zone authority and boundary-change criteria from primary
  sources.
- Review selected Federal Register time-zone boundary proceedings.
- Map DOT criteria to measurable proxy variables.
- Compare solar-first rankings against convenience-of-commerce proxy indicators.

## Evaluation

- **Baselines**:
  - solar-error-only ranking,
  - current legal assignment,
  - DOT proxy feasibility layer.
- **Success criteria**:
  - every DOT criterion has a source-backed measurable proxy or an explicit
    "not currently measurable" note;
  - examples show how proxy evidence can support, weaken, or block a solar-fit
    recommendation;
  - civil-time policy role accepts current-law/proposed-law distinction.
- **Failure modes to test**:
  - counties split by state/media/economic regions,
  - low-data rural counties,
  - cross-border metro regions,
  - active legislation mistaken for current law.
